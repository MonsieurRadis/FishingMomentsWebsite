H2: Why Catch and Release Matters
- Fish populations are under pressure from overfishing, pollution, habitat loss
- C&R allows anglers to enjoy fishing while preserving stocks
- Done properly, survival rates are 90-95%+
- Done poorly, fish die hours or days after release
- It's our responsibility as anglers

H2: Before You Fish: Set Up for Success
- Use barbless hooks (or crimp barbs flat) — faster, cleaner unhooking
- Have your unhooking tools ready: forceps, pliers, disgorger
- Prepare your landing net — a rubberized mesh is gentlest on fish
- Have a wet unhooking mat if fishing for larger fish (carp, pike)
- Don't use stainless steel hooks (they don't dissolve if a fish breaks off)

H2: During the Fight
- Don't play fish to exhaustion — use appropriate tackle strength
- Bring fish in steadily, don't let them fight for 10+ minutes unnecessarily
- Oversized gear is better than undersized for C&R (shorter fight)
- In warm water (20°C+): this is critical — exhausted fish in warm water die

H2: Handling the Fish
- WET your hands before touching ANY fish — dry hands strip their slime coat
- Support the fish horizontally — never hold by the jaw alone (except bass)
- Keep fingers away from gills — gill damage is often fatal
- Minimize air exposure: 30 seconds max out of water
- If photographing: prepare the camera FIRST, then lift the fish briefly
- Hold fish low over water or mat — dropping from height = internal injuries

H2: Unhooking
- Lip-hooked fish: quick and easy, gently back the hook out
- Deep-hooked fish: use a disgorger or long forceps, DON'T pull
- If the hook is in the gills or stomach: cut the line close — the hook will corrode out
- Trying to remove a deep hook does more damage than leaving it
- Barbless hooks make ALL unhooking easier and safer

H2: Releasing the Fish
- Hold the fish upright in the water, facing into any current
- Support the belly — let the fish recover its balance
- Wait until the fish kicks away under its own power
- Don't throw or drop fish back in
- In rivers: release in calm water, not the main current (tired fish get swept away)
- If the fish rolls belly-up: gently right it and keep supporting

H2: Temperature Considerations
- Warm water (above 20°C): fish are already stressed, handle even faster
- Consider not fishing for sensitive species (trout) in very warm water
- Cold water: fish recover slower but are less stressed
- Air temperature matters too — don't lay fish on hot ground or rocks

H2: Keepnets, Sacks, and Retaining
- If you must retain fish: use a large keepnet in deep water
- Don't keep fish in small containers or buckets
- Carp sacks: only in cool water, for short periods
- Best practice: catch, unhook, photograph, release — no retaining needed

Catch and Release: Best Practices for Fish Survival

Catch and release fishing is one of the most important habits modern anglers can adopt.

It allows us to enjoy the sport, target large fish, and preserve healthy populations for the future. But catch and release only works when it is done correctly.

Done properly, survival rates can exceed 90–95%.

Done poorly, fish may swim away — only to die hours or days later from stress, injury, or infection.

Ethical fishing is not just about letting fish go. It is about ensuring they survive.

H2: Why Catch and Release Matters

Fish populations across Europe face real pressure.

- Overfishing in some regions  
- Pollution and agricultural runoff  
- Habitat destruction  
- Rising water temperatures  

Catch and release fishing helps reduce harvest pressure while still allowing anglers to experience the sport.

Large predator fish such as pike, carp, and trout are especially valuable breeders. Releasing them maintains genetic strength and population stability.

However, the responsibility sits with us.

If fish are handled poorly — dropped on rocks, suffocated in air, squeezed improperly — delayed mortality increases dramatically.

The goal is not just release.

The goal is survival.

H2: Before You Fish: Set Up for Success

Successful catch and release tips begin before the first cast.

Barbless Hooks

Use barbless hooks whenever possible, or crimp the barbs flat with pliers.

Barbless hooks:

- Reduce tissue damage  
- Speed up unhooking  
- Minimize handling time  

A hook that slides out easily is far safer for the fish.

Unhooking Tools Ready

Have forceps, pliers, or a disgorger prepared and within reach.

Searching through your bag while a fish lies exposed increases stress.

Landing Net Choice

Use a rubberized or knotless mesh net.

Traditional nylon nets remove protective slime and can damage fins.

Unhooking Mat (For Larger Fish)

If targeting carp or pike, use a wet unhooking mat.

Never place large fish directly on dry grass, gravel, or rocks.

Hook Material

Avoid stainless steel hooks.

If a fish breaks off with a stainless hook embedded, it may not corrode naturally. Standard carbon steel hooks degrade over time.

Preparation reduces risk before the fight even begins.

H2: During the Fight

How you fight a fish determines how well it survives.

Do Not Play Fish to Exhaustion

Long fights build lactic acid in fish muscle tissue.

An exhausted fish in warm water may not recover.

Use appropriate tackle strength.

Oversized Gear Is Safer Than Undersized

Using slightly heavier line and tackle shortens fight time.

Bringing a fish in steadily is better than allowing a 10–15 minute struggle.

In warm water (above 20°C), this becomes critical.

Fish already face oxygen stress in summer. Prolonged fights dramatically increase mortality risk.

For more on high-temperature concerns, see #12 Summer Fishing and #03 Trout Temperature.

A shorter, controlled fight equals a stronger release.

H2: Handling the Fish

Fish handling tips are simple — but vital.

Wet Your Hands

Always wet your hands before touching a fish.

Fish are coated in a protective slime layer that defends against infection and parasites.

Dry hands remove this barrier.

Support the Fish Properly

Support fish horizontally.

Use one hand under the belly and one supporting near the tail.

Never hold fish vertically by the jaw alone (with the exception of bass species that are anatomically suited to lip holding).

Avoid the Gills

Never place fingers inside gills.

Gill tissue is delicate and damage is often fatal.

Minimize Air Exposure

Keep air exposure under 30 seconds.

Prepare your camera first.
Lift the fish briefly.
Take the photo.
Return it to water.

Every additional second out of water increases stress.

Hold Fish Low

When photographing, hold fish close to the water or mat.

Dropping a fish from even half a meter can cause internal injuries.

The golden rule of catch and release fishing:

Water is home. Keep them in it as much as possible.

H2: Unhooking

Lip-Hooked Fish

These are ideal.

Back the hook out gently.
Barbless hooks make this nearly effortless.

Deep-Hooked Fish

Use long forceps or a disgorger.

Do not pull aggressively.

If the hook is deeply embedded in the throat or stomach, cut the line as close as possible and leave the hook.

Attempting to remove a deep hook often causes catastrophic internal damage.

Carbon hooks corrode naturally over time.

Gill-Hooked Fish

If bleeding heavily from the gills, survival chances decrease.

Minimize handling.
Release immediately.

Trying to surgically remove a hook from gill arches usually worsens injury.

Barbless hooks dramatically reduce deep hooking incidents.

Ethical fishing begins with prevention.

H2: Releasing the Fish

Releasing properly is the final — and crucial — step.

Hold Upright

Place the fish upright in the water.

Support the belly.

Face Into Current (If in River)

In rivers, hold the fish facing upstream.

Water flowing through the gills accelerates oxygen recovery.

Do Not Push Back and Forth

Do not “pump” the fish aggressively.

Let water flow naturally.

Wait for Strength

When the fish kicks strongly and maintains balance, release your support.

Do not throw or drop fish.

If Fish Rolls Belly-Up

Gently right it and continue supporting until it regains equilibrium.

In rivers, release fish in calmer water near the bank — not directly into strong current.

A tired fish swept downstream may not survive.

How to release fish safely often determines whether catch and release truly works.

H2: Temperature Considerations

Temperature changes everything.

Warm Water (Above 20°C)

Fish experience oxygen stress.

Handle even faster.
Shorten fights.
Reduce air exposure to absolute minimum.

For sensitive species such as trout, consider not fishing when water exceeds safe limits (see #03 Trout Temperature).

Cold Water

Cold water holds more oxygen.

Fish recover more slowly but are less physiologically stressed.

Air Temperature

Never lay fish on hot rocks, dry sand, or frozen ground.

Extreme surfaces damage skin and internal tissue.

If surfaces are hot or icy, keep fish in water at all times.

Ethical fishing means adjusting behavior to seasonal conditions.

H2: Keepnets, Sacks, and Retaining

Retention increases stress.

If using a keepnet:

- Choose a large diameter net  
- Place it in deep, well-oxygenated water  

Never overcrowd fish.

Buckets and Small Containers

Avoid them entirely.

Oxygen depletion and stress increase rapidly in confined spaces.

Carp Sacks

Use only in cool water and for very short periods.

Prolonged retention — especially in warm conditions — is harmful.

Best Practice

Catch.
Unhook.
Photograph.
Release.

No retention required.

Modern catch and release fishing values fish survival over trophy possession.

H2: Teaching Responsible Angling

If you fish with children or beginners, demonstrate correct fish handling from the start.

For a full beginner overview, see #15 Freshwater Fishing for Beginners.

Habits learned early last a lifetime.

Normalize:

- Wet hands  
- Short air exposure  
- Barbless hooks  
- Quick releases  

Ethical fishing culture protects future fishing opportunities.

Conclusion

Catch and release fishing is powerful — but only if practiced properly.

Prepare before fishing.
Fight fish efficiently.
Handle gently.
Minimize air exposure.
Release with care.
Adjust for temperature.
